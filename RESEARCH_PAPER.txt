1. ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Public address (PA) systems in transportation environments — railway
stations, airports, metro terminals, and bus rapid transit hubs — serve as
critical safety communication channels responsible for conveying platform
assignments, train delays, cancellations, and emergency evacuation
instructions. Despite their operational importance, the quality of audio
announcements delivered through these systems is rarely measured
systematically. Existing reference-based quality metrics such as PESQ
(Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality, ITU-T P.862) and POLQA
(Perceptual Objective Listening Quality Assessment, ITU-T P.863) require
clean reference recordings and specialized hardware, making them impractical
for lightweight, real-time field deployment.

This paper presents the Announcement Quality Analyzer (AQA) — a blind,
non-intrusive audio evaluation system that estimates speech quality using a
deterministic Digital Signal Processing (DSP) pipeline requiring no reference
signals, no GPU infrastructure, and no deep learning model weights. The
system extracts four acoustic features — Windowed Signal-to-Noise Ratio
(wSNR), Spectral Flatness (SF), Formant Energy Ratio (FER), and Zero
Crossing Rate (ZCR) — from one-second temporal windows of input audio.
These features are transformed through non-linear mathematical mappings
(sigmoid, power, logarithmic) and combined using a multiplicative penalty
architecture.

The resulting quality score, on a 0-to-100 scale, consistently segregates
clean studio-quality recordings (score >= 86) from heavily distorted or
noise-dominated audio (score < 1). The system underwent five complete
iterations of the scoring model before achieving this separation. It is
deployed as a full-stack web application with a Next.js frontend and a
FastAPI Python backend, requiring only CPU-based NumPy and SciPy
dependencies, making it suitable for deployment in infrastructure-
constrained environments including single-board computers.

2. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

2.1 BACKGROUND

The intelligibility of public address announcements is a well-established
determinant of passenger safety and user experience in large-scale transit
systems. The International Telecommunication Union recognizes speech
transmission quality as a core service metric through its G.107 E-model
standard [1], which models the conversational quality of voice networks
based on impairment factors including noise, packet loss, delay, and codec
distortion.

In practice, however, the monitoring of PA system quality in transportation
environments remains almost entirely manual. Station supervisors are
responsible for periodic spot-checks — listening to selected recordings and
filing subjective quality reports. This approach fails on three axes:

First, it does not scale. India's railway network alone comprises over 7,000
stations carrying 22 million passengers daily. Manual auditing at this scale
is operationally infeasible.

Second, it is subjective. Two supervisors listening to the same recording
will often produce different quality assessments. There is no standardized
internal metric or scoring rubric.

Third, it is reactive. By the time a poor-quality announcement is identified,
the passengers who needed that information have already missed it.

Objective speech quality metrics have been developed with significant rigor
in the telecommunications domain. The ITU-T P.862 standard (PESQ —
Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality) [2] and its successor P.863
(POLQA — Perceptual Objective Listening Quality Assessment) [6] are the de
facto reference-based metrics for assessing narrowband and wideband
telephony quality. Both produce a predicted Mean Opinion Score (MOS) on the
1-to-5 scale used in subjective listening tests.

However, both PESQ and POLQA have two fundamental requirements that make
them impractical for PA system monitoring:

1. They require a clean reference recording — the "ideal" version of the
   degraded signal. In a live station environment, no such reference exists.
   Each announcement is unique and spontaneous.

2. They require specialized licensed software and calibrated audio hardware
   — typically costing thousands of dollars per installation.

Non-intrusive (blind) quality estimation eliminates the reference
requirement. The ITU-T P.563 standard [3] and more recent deep learning
approaches (e.g., NISQA by Mittag et al. [4]) operate on single-ended
signals. However, P.563 was designed for telephony-specific degradations
and performs poorly in high-ambient-noise PA environments. Deep learning
approaches require GPU hardware and pre-trained model weights, introducing
deployment constraints.


2.2 MOTIVATION

This work is motivated by the operational gap between the criticality of PA
system quality and the absence of lightweight, automated evaluation tools.

The goal is to develop a fully blind quality estimator — one that requires
no reference signal, no deep learning infrastructure, no GPU, and no
specialized audio hardware — yet produces scores that are:

(a) Perceptually meaningful: A score of 86 should correspond to audio
    that a human listener would call "clear and intelligible." A score of 5
    should correspond to audio that a listener would call "unintelligible."

(b) Sufficiently discriminative: The system must produce a large numerical
    gap between good and bad audio. A gap of 10 points on a 100-point scale
    is not operationally useful — an operator glancing at a dashboard cannot
    distinguish "42" from "38" as meaningful categories. The target is a gap
    of at least 50 points.

(c) Deterministic: The same audio file must always produce the same score.
    No randomness, no stochastic sampling, no model variability.

(d) Explainable: An operator must be able to understand why a particular
    score was assigned. The system must expose intermediate metrics and
    penalty factors.

(e) Deployable on minimal hardware: The system must run on a standard
    computer or single-board device (e.g., Raspberry Pi 4) without
    requiring internet connectivity for the core scoring function.


2.3 CONTRIBUTIONS

This paper makes the following contributions:

1. A four-feature DSP extraction pipeline (wSNR, SF, FER, ZCR) with
   one-second temporal windowing and a formant-variance-based confidence
   score.

2. A non-linear scoring engine using sigmoid, power, and logarithmic
   transformations calibrated to perceptual acoustic properties of human
   speech.

3. A multiplicative penalty architecture that creates true feature
   interaction, producing score spreads exceeding 85 points between clean
   and corrupted audio — significantly outperforming additive approaches.

4. A complete full-stack web deployment including real-time microphone
   capture, live waveform visualization, and a transparent debug panel
   exposing all intermediate scoring parameters.

5. A systematic record of five experimental model iterations, including
   failure analysis for each rejected approach.

3. LITERATURE AND APPROACH OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Non-intrusive speech quality estimation has gained significant attention
over the past two decades. The approaches can be broadly categorized into
three families:

3.1 SIGNAL-BASED METHODS

ANIQUE+ (Auditory Non-Intrusive Quality Estimation Plus) operates on a
psychoacoustic auditory model, processing audio through a simulated
cochlear filter bank and computing modulation-domain features. It produces
MOS predictions without a reference signal but was designed for and
calibrated against telephony-grade speech, not PA system audio with heavy
ambient interference.

ITU-T P.563 is the standardized single-ended speech quality metric. It
combines multiple signal analysis modules (voice activity detection, SNR
estimation, speech level analysis, noise analysis, and unnatural speech
detection) into a composite model. However, its training data and
coefficient tables are optimized for narrowband telephony (300-3400 Hz,
G.711 codec) and its performance degrades significantly on wideband PA
recordings with environmental noise profiles (crowd chatter, train rumble,
wind) not represented in its training corpus.

3.2 LEARNING-BASED METHODS

Mittag et al. [4] proposed NISQA — a deep CNN–self-attention architecture
trained on crowdsourced MOS datasets. NISQA operates on mel-spectrograms
and achieves Pearson correlation coefficients above 0.93 against human
ratings on multiple benchmark sets. However, NISQA requires a GPU-capable
inference environment, a pre-trained model file of several hundred
megabytes, and PyTorch as a runtime dependency.

Catellier and Voran [5] explored using handcrafted features (spectral
envelope, temporal envelope, modulation spectrum) as input to a random
forest regressor, achieving competitive MOS prediction without neural
network infrastructure. This approach is closer to our philosophy but
still requires a labeled training corpus for the regressor.

3.3 HYBRID DSP-STATISTICAL METHODS

SRMR (Speech-to-Reverberation Modulation energy Ratio) uses modulation
spectrum analysis to estimate the ratio of direct speech energy to
reverberant energy. It is computationally efficient and correlates well
with perceived reverberance, but is limited to a single degradation
dimension — it cannot assess broadband noise, clipping, or microphone
distortion.

3.4 OUR POSITIONING

The AQA system draws from the signal-based tradition — engineering acoustic
features from first principles — but replaces empirical regression
(as in ANIQUE+ and P.563) with a hand-calibrated non-linear scoring function
specifically designed for the high-noise, time-varying, reverberant
conditions of railway PA environments. Unlike learning-based methods, it
requires no training data, no model weights, and no GPU. Unlike P.563, its
feature set and thresholds are tuned for PA-specific degradation profiles.

4. METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

4.1 SYSTEM OVERVIEW

The complete data flow architecture is:

    Input Audio (WAV/MP3/M4A)
        |
        v
    Format Standardization          [Backend: PyDub -> 16kHz mono PCM16]
        |
        v
    Amplitude Normalization         [Processor: peak normalization to [-1,+1]]
        |
        v
    Soft Silence Trimming           [Processor: energy-gated boundary detection]
        |
        v
    1-Second Temporal Chunking      [Processor: non-overlapping windows]
        |
        v
    Per-Chunk Feature Extraction    [Processor: wSNR, SF, FER, ZCR]
        |
        v
    Temporal Aggregation            [Processor: mean + std -> confidence]
        |
        v
    Non-Linear Scoring Engine       [Inference: sigmoid + power + log + gates]
        |
        v
    Quality Classification          [Inference: 5-tier labeling]
        |
        v
    Feedback Generation             [Inference: threshold-based NL messages]
        |
        v
    JSON API Response               [Backend: FastAPI AnalysisResponse]
        |
        v
    Dashboard Visualization         [Frontend: Next.js gauge + metric panels]


4.2 FORMAT STANDARDIZATION

Uploaded audio (WAV, MP3, or M4A) is converted to a canonical format using
the PyDub audio manipulation library:

    audio_seg = AudioSegment.from_file(input_path)
    audio_seg = audio_seg.set_channels(1).set_frame_rate(16000)
    audio_seg.export(output_path, format="wav",
                     parameters=["-acodec", "pcm_s16le"])

This ensures all downstream processing operates on 16-bit signed integer
PCM samples at 16,000 Hz in a single (mono) channel. The choice of 16 kHz
matches the Nyquist criterion for the 300-3400 Hz speech band (Nyquist
frequency = 8000 Hz, which exceeds the 3400 Hz upper band).


4.3 PRE-PROCESSING

4.3.1 Peak Amplitude Normalization

    if max(|x[n]|) > 0:
        x_norm[n] = x[n] / max(|x[n]|)

This scales all recordings to a common amplitude range of [-1.0, +1.0],
removing volume differences caused by varying microphone gains.

4.3.2 Soft Silence Trimming

Rather than binary energy masking — which was used in an earlier iteration
and found to destroy waveform continuity by removing individual low-energy
samples within speech segments — the final system uses boundary-based soft
trimming:

    energy = x[n]^2
    threshold = 0.01
    active_indices = {n : energy[n] > threshold}
    start = max(0, min(active_indices) - 2000)
    end = min(N, max(active_indices) + 2000)
    trimmed = x[start:end]

The 2000-sample padding (125 ms at 16 kHz) preserves attack transients and
release tails of speech, preventing the artificial sharpening of boundaries
that caused the earlier binary mask approach to produce inflated ZCR values.


4.4 FEATURE EXTRACTION

All features are computed independently for each one-second chunk (16,000
samples at 16 kHz). The per-chunk results are aggregated after all chunks
have been processed.

4.4.1 Windowed Signal-to-Noise Ratio (wSNR)

The audio chunk is subdivided into non-overlapping 20 ms frames (320 samples
at 16 kHz). Frame-level mean squared energy is computed:

    E_k = (1/L) * sum_{n=1}^{L} x_k[n]^2      for frame k of length L

The frame energies are sorted in ascending order. The bottom 20% represent
background noise energy. The top 20% represent speech energy:

    E_noise  = mean(E_sorted[1 : floor(0.2 * K)])
    E_speech = mean(E_sorted[ceil(0.8 * K) : K])

The Windowed SNR is:

    wSNR = 10 * log10((E_speech + epsilon) / (E_noise + epsilon))

where epsilon = 1e-10 prevents division by zero and log(0) errors.

This frame-sorting approach is more robust than percentile-based methods
(which were used in an earlier iteration) because it directly identifies
acoustic regimes rather than relying on amplitude percentiles that can be
distorted by transient impulsive noise.

4.4.2 Spectral Flatness (SF)

The Fast Fourier Transform is applied to each chunk:

    X[k] = FFT(x[n])     for k = 0, 1, ..., N-1

The positive-frequency power spectrum is:

    P[k] = |X[k]|^2      for k = 0, 1, ..., N/2 - 1

An epsilon of 1e-10 is added to prevent log(0).

Spectral Flatness is defined as the ratio of the geometric mean to the
arithmetic mean of the power spectrum:

    SF = G(P) / A(P)

where:
    A(P) = (1/M) * sum_{k=1}^{M} P[k]
    G(P) = exp((1/M) * sum_{k=1}^{M} log(P[k]))

By the inequality of arithmetic and geometric means (AM-GM inequality),
0 <= SF <= 1. SF = 1 indicates white noise (uniform spectral distribution).
SF -> 0 indicates tonal content (spectral energy concentrated at specific
frequencies).

4.4.3 Formant Energy Ratio (FER)

Using the same FFT power spectrum P[k] and the corresponding frequency
axis:

    f[k] = k * (f_s / N)     where f_s = 16000 Hz

A boolean mask selects the speech band:

    M_speech[k] = True   if 300 <= f[k] <= 3400
                  False   otherwise

The Formant Energy Ratio is:

    FER = sum(P[k] * M_speech[k]) / (sum(P[k]) + epsilon)

For clean human speech, FER typically falls in the range [0.35, 0.50].
The ratio does not reach 1.0 because the human voice produces energy
outside the 300-3400 Hz band: fundamental frequency (F0) for male speakers
is typically 85-180 Hz, and fricative consonants produce energy up to
8000 Hz.

Environmental noise sources — fan hum, traffic rumble, crowd chatter,
electrical interference — distribute spectral energy more broadly, pulling
FER below 0.2.

4.4.4 Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR)

    ZCR = (1/N) * |{n : sign(x[n]) != sign(x[n-1])}|

Computed efficiently as:

    ZCR = len(nonzero(diff(x > 0))) / len(x)

Clean speech ZCR typically occupies [0.06, 0.12]. Values above 0.15
correlate with electrical hiss, microphone clipping, and wind buffeting.
Values above 0.30 indicate severe signal corruption.

4.4.5 Temporal Aggregation and Confidence Score

After all one-second chunks are processed, the four metrics are averaged:

    wSNR_final = mean(wSNR_i)
    SF_final   = mean(SF_i)
    FER_final  = mean(FER_i)
    ZCR_final  = mean(ZCR_i)

A confidence score is derived from the temporal consistency of FER:

    sigma_FER = std(FER_i)
    Confidence = clip(1.0 - 2.5 * sigma_FER, 0.1, 1.0)

High FER variance indicates alternating speech and noise segments,
suggesting the recording is unstable. The confidence score provides an
additional reliability indicator — it does not affect the quality score
computation but is reported to the operator.


4.5 SCORING ENGINE — MATHEMATICAL FORMULATION

4.5.1 Non-Linear Base Score (Maximum 100 Points)

COMPONENT A: wSNR Score (0 to 40 points) — Sigmoid Scaling

    wSNR_score = 40 / (1 + exp(-0.5 * (wSNR - 12)))

The sigmoid function approximates the Weber-Fechner law of sensory
perception: perceived sensation is proportional to the logarithm of
stimulus intensity. The center is placed at 12 dB, and the steepness
coefficient is 0.5, producing:

    wSNR =  3 dB  ->  score =  0.4 points
    wSNR = 12 dB  ->  score = 20.0 points
    wSNR = 18 dB  ->  score = 38.1 points
    wSNR = 22 dB  ->  score = 39.7 points

COMPONENT B: Formant Energy Score (0 to 35 points) — Power Scaling

    FER_norm = clip(FER / 0.45, 0, 1)
    Formant_score = 35 * FER_norm^1.5

The normalization by 0.45 accounts for the biological ceiling of human
vocal formant energy concentration. The super-linear exponent (1.5) rewards
speech-dominant audio disproportionately:

    FER = 0.10  ->  score =  2.6 points
    FER = 0.30  ->  score = 16.2 points
    FER = 0.45  ->  score = 35.0 points
    FER = 0.50  ->  score = 35.0 points  (clamped)

COMPONENT C: Spectral Flatness Score (0 to 25 points) — Logarithmic Scaling

    SF_clipped = clip(SF, 1e-6, 1.0)
    SF_dB = -10 * log10(SF_clipped)
    SF_score = 25 * clip(SF_dB / 30, 0, 1)

Converting to decibels linearizes the perceptual tonality dimension. The
ceiling of 30 dB provides resolution across the practically relevant
flatness range:

    SF = 0.04  ->  SF_dB = 14.0  ->  score = 11.7 points
    SF = 0.08  ->  SF_dB = 11.0  ->  score =  9.1 points
    SF = 0.20  ->  SF_dB =  7.0  ->  score =  5.8 points
    SF = 0.55  ->  SF_dB =  2.6  ->  score =  2.2 points

BASE TOTAL:

    Base = wSNR_score + Formant_score + SF_score

Maximum theoretical Base = 40 + 35 + 25 = 100. In practice, clean studio
audio achieves approximately 86 points.

4.5.2 Multiplicative Penalty Gates

PENALTY A: ZCR Exponential Decay (P_zcr)

    if ZCR <= 0.12:
        P_zcr = 1.0                        (safe zone — no penalty)
    else:
        P_zcr = exp(-8.0 * (ZCR - 0.12))

    P_zcr = clip(P_zcr, 0, 1)

The safe zone of 0.12 protects natural consonant sounds ("s", "sh", "f")
from triggering the penalty. The steep decay constant of -8.0 produces:

    ZCR = 0.12  ->  P_zcr = 1.00
    ZCR = 0.15  ->  P_zcr = 0.79
    ZCR = 0.18  ->  P_zcr = 0.62
    ZCR = 0.25  ->  P_zcr = 0.35
    ZCR = 0.35  ->  P_zcr = 0.16

PENALTY B: Noise Trapdoor (P_noise)

    if wSNR < 10 dB:       P_noise = 0.4   (60% instant collapse)
    elif SF > 0.2:          P_noise = 0.5   (50% collapse)
    else:                   P_noise = 1.0   (no penalty)

This is a hard gating function designed to create dramatic score separation.
If the audio demonstrates catastrophically low SNR or undeniable broadband
noise (high flatness), the base score is immediately halved or worse.

PENALTY C: Speech Presence Gate (P_speech)

    P_speech = min(1.0, (FER / 0.35)^0.7)

The compressive exponent of 0.7 produces a generous gate: audio with FER
of 0.35 already achieves P_speech = 1.0. Only severely speech-deficient
recordings (FER < 0.20) are meaningfully penalized.

4.5.3 Final Score Pipeline

    Final = Base * (0.5 + 0.5 * P_speech) * P_zcr * P_noise
    Final = clip(Final, 0, 100)

The soft floor of 0.5 in the speech gate is critical. Without it, audio
with zero formant content would be multiplied by zero — regardless of how
acoustically clean the signal might be. With the floor, the speech gate
reduces the score by at most 50% — still a severe penalty, but one that
allows non-speech audio (such as station alerts or tonal chimes) to
receive a non-zero quality assessment.

4.5.4 Quality Classification

    Score >= 80:  "Excellent"
    Score >= 60:  "Good"
    Score >= 40:  "Average"
    Score >= 20:  "Poor"
    Score <  20:  "Bad"

5. EXPERIMENTAL METHODS — ALL APPROACHES TRIED
================================================================================

This section documents every scoring methodology attempted during
development, including those that were ultimately rejected.


5.1 METHOD 1: DIRECT LINEAR MAPPING

Description:
Each DSP feature was mapped linearly between a minimum and maximum boundary:

    score = clip((val - min_val) / (max_val - min_val) * max_pts, 0, max_pts)

Parameters:
    wSNR:    min=5,    max=22,   max_pts=40
    SF:      min=0.35, max=0.05, max_pts=25  (inverted)
    FER:     min=0.2,  max=0.6,  max_pts=35
    ZCR:     min=0.12, max=0.35, max_pts=30  (penalty)

Final score = wSNR_score + SF_score + FER_score - ZCR_penalty

Why Considered:
Simplest possible implementation. Easy to understand, debug, and explain.
No advanced mathematics required.

Result:
Clean audio scored approximately 40-50 out of 100. Noisy audio scored
approximately 30-40. The score gap was only 10-15 points — not visually
distinguishable on a dashboard gauge.

Limitation / Why Rejected:
Human perception of audio quality is fundamentally non-linear. A linear
mapping fails to reward high-quality audio strongly enough and does not
punish poor-quality audio severely enough. The ZCR subtraction was too
aggressive for clean speech containing fricative consonants, unfairly
penalizing high-quality recordings.


5.2 METHOD 2: LINEAR SCORING WITH SUBTRACTIVE NOISE PENALTIES

Description:
Extended Method 1 by adding conditional penalty deductions:

    noise_penalty = 0
    if SNR < 10:       noise_penalty += 20
    if flatness > 0.2: noise_penalty += 15

    final = base_score - zcr_penalty - noise_penalty

Why Considered:
Intuitive extension. Preserves the linear base logic while introducing
noise sensitivity through hard thresholds.

Result:
Clean audio scored approximately 40-65. Noisy audio scored approximately
20-35. Better separation than Method 1, but still insufficient.

Limitation / Why Rejected:
Subtraction does not interact with the base scale. A recording with a base
score of 60 and a recording with a base score of 30 both suffer the same
20-point SNR penalty — but the perceptual impact is completely different.
Clean audio with naturally elevated ZCR (fricative-heavy speech) continued
to receive excessive penalties. Subtraction allowed noisy audio to
partially "recover" if one feature happened to score well.


5.3 METHOD 3: NON-LINEAR MAPPING WITH SUBTRACTIVE PENALTIES

Description:
Introduced sigmoid scaling for SNR, power scaling for FER, and inverse
quadratic scaling for SF:

    wSNR_score = 40 / (1 + exp(-0.4 * (SNR - 18)))
    FER_score  = 35 * (FER ^ 1.5)
    SF_score   = 25 * (1 - SF) ^ 2

Retained the subtractive penalty structure from Method 2:

    final = base - zcr_penalty - noise_penalty

Why Considered:
Non-linear scoring functions should better approximate perceptual quality
curves, resolving the flat-scoring issue of Methods 1 and 2.

Result:
Clean audio scored approximately 50-70. Improved separation, but the
penalty subtraction continued to drag down scores for audio that should
have been classified as high-quality.

Limitation / Why Rejected:
Three specific issues:

(a) Sigmoid center at 18 dB was too high. Clean audio SNR of 20-22 dB
    only produced scores of 27-33 out of 40 — failing to saturate.

(b) Inverse quadratic flatness ((1-SF)^2) was too sensitive to the raw
    SF value. Small changes in SF near 0.3 produced disproportionately
    large score swings.

(c) Subtraction remained structurally problematic. The ZCR penalty was
    independent of the base score level.


5.4 METHOD 4: MULTIPLICATIVE PENALTY ARCHITECTURE (FIRST ATTEMPT)

Description:
Replaced subtraction with multiplication:

    p_zcr = exp(-3.0 * (ZCR - 0.12))     clamped [0, 1]
    p_noise = 0.4 if SNR < 10 else (0.5 if SF > 0.2 else 1.0)
    p_speech = FER                        (raw ratio, 0 to 1)

    final = base * p_zcr * p_noise * p_speech

Why Considered:
Multiplicative gates create true feature interaction. A recording cannot
compensate for catastrophic noise by being slightly better on another
dimension. Each multiplier independently constrains the ceiling.

Result:
Noisy audio correctly collapsed to near-zero. However, clean audio also
collapsed to 20-30 out of 100.

Limitation / Why Rejected:
Critical bug identified: P_speech = raw FER. Since human speech never
achieves FER > 0.50 (due to sub-fundamental bass energy and high-frequency
fricatives), the speech gate was inadvertently capping ALL scores at
approximately 50% of base. A clean recording with base score 80 and
FER = 0.45 would produce final = 80 * 0.45 * 1.0 * 1.0 = 36.

Additionally, the ZCR decay constant of -3.0 was too gentle — it did not
distinguish moderate from severe distortion effectively. The sigmoid center
at 18 dB persisted from Method 3.


5.5 METHOD 5: CALIBRATED MULTIPLICATIVE ARCHITECTURE WITH dB FLATNESS
    (FINAL MODEL)

Description:
Addressed all identified issues from Methods 1-4:

(a) Sigmoid SNR: Center shifted from 18 dB to 12 dB. Steepness increased
    from 0.4 to 0.5. This allows clean audio (18-22 dB) to fully saturate
    the SNR score component.

(b) Formant normalization: Changed from raw FER to FER/0.45. This
    biological calibration allows perfect human speech to achieve full
    points.

(c) Spectral Flatness: Replaced inverse quadratic with log-dB
    transformation. SF_dB = -10*log10(SF), scored as SF_dB/30. This
    linearizes the perceptual tonality dimension.

(d) Speech gate: Replaced raw p_speech = FER with:
    P_speech = min(1, (FER/0.35)^0.7)
    Applied with soft floor: (0.5 + 0.5 * P_speech)

(e) ZCR: Added safe zone (ZCR <= 0.12 -> P_zcr = 1.0). Increased
    exponential decay from -3.0 to -8.0 for ZCR above the safe zone.

(f) Pre-processing: Replaced hard binary mask trimming with soft boundary
    trimming (2000-sample contextual padding). Replaced percentile-based
    SNR with frame-sorting method.

Result:
    Clean studio audio:         86.4   (Excellent)
    Good railway announcement:  76.9   (Good)
    Moderate ambient noise:     27.6   (Poor)
    Noisy / distorted:           0.5   (Bad)
    Heavy noise, no speech:      0.0   (Bad)

Score gap: 86.4 points between best and worst cases.

Why Selected:
This model produces perceptually correct, large-spread scores that map to
actionable quality classifications. The multiplicative architecture ensures
no single feature can compensate for catastrophic failure in another. The
soft floor on the speech gate prevents valid but formant-weak audio from
being unfairly zeroed.

6. FINAL MODEL SUMMARY
================================================================================

The final scoring model uses a two-stage architecture: a Non-Linear Base
Score (max 100 points) followed by a Multiplicative Penalty Matrix.

The Non-Linear Base Score combines three independently computed component
scores, each using a different mathematical transformation chosen to align
with the perceptual properties of its corresponding acoustic feature:

    - Sigmoid for SNR (Weber-Fechner-like perception)
    - Power function for FER (super-linear reward for speech presence)
    - Logarithmic dB for SF (linearization of the multiplicative tonality
      dimension)

The Multiplicative Penalty Matrix applies three independent gates:

    - ZCR exponential decay with a safe zone
    - Binary noise trapdoor based on SNR and SF thresholds
    - Compressive speech presence gate with a soft floor

Design Principle: Why Multiplicative is Superior to Additive

In an additive (subtractive) system, the final score is:

    Final = Base - Penalty_1 - Penalty_2 - Penalty_3

This means a recording with Base = 90 and Penalty_1 = 20 scores 70. But a
recording with Base = 30 and Penalty_1 = 20 scores only 10. The same
absolute penalty produces drastically different relative impacts depending
on the base — which is mathematically inconsistent.

In a multiplicative system:

    Final = Base * P_1 * P_2 * P_3

A recording with Base = 90 and P_1 = 0.5 scores 45. A recording with
Base = 30 and P_1 = 0.5 scores 15. The relative impact (50% reduction), is
consistent regardless of the base level. This is perceptually correct —
a degradation should reduce quality by a proportional amount, not a fixed
amount.

7. RESULTS AND ANALYSIS
================================================================================

7.1 SCORE DISTRIBUTION ACROSS AUDIO PROFILES

Audio Condition            | wSNR  | FER  | SF   | ZCR  | Base | Final | Label
---------------------------+-------+------+------+------+------+-------+--------
Clean Studio Recording     | 22 dB | 0.50 | 0.04 | 0.09 | 86.4 | 86.4  | Excellent
Good Railway Announcement  | 18 dB | 0.42 | 0.08 | 0.11 | 78.8 | 76.9  | Good
Moderate Ambient Noise     | 13 dB | 0.30 | 0.15 | 0.18 | 50.8 | 27.6  | Poor
Noisy / Distorted          |  7 dB | 0.12 | 0.35 | 0.35 | 11.7 |  0.5  | Bad
Heavy Noise, No Speech     |  3 dB | 0.05 | 0.55 | 0.50 |  3.9 |  0.0  | Bad


7.2 KEY OBSERVATIONS

Observation 1: Strong Category Separation
The score gap between Clean Studio (86.4) and Heavy Noise (0.0) is 86.4
points on a 100-point scale. This exceeds our design target of 50 points.

Observation 2: Multiplicative Compounding
For the "Noisy/Distorted" profile, the base score is 11.7 — already low.
But the three multipliers compound: 0.5 * 0.16 * 0.4 = 0.032. The base of
11.7 is further crushed by a factor of 0.032, producing a final score of
0.5. Without the multiplicative architecture (i.e., using subtraction), this
profile would have scored approximately 11.7 - 20 = 0, achieving the same
endpoint but providing no intermediate resolution.

Observation 3: Sigmoid Saturation Behavior
The sigmoid SNR component produces 39.7 out of 40 for clean audio (22 dB)
but only 0.4 out of 40 for heavy noise (3 dB). This 39-point swing in a
single feature component is the primary driver of base score separation.

Observation 4: The Speech Gate Calibration Challenge
In Method 4, using raw FER as a multiplier capped clean audio at ~50% of
its base score. The fix — normalizing by 0.35, applying a compressive
exponent of 0.7, and adding a soft floor — was the most consequential
single design decision in the project. It took two iterations (Methods 4
and 5) to arrive at this formulation.


7.3 REAL-WORLD IMPLICATIONS

The 60-point threshold (classification "Good") maps to a practical station
deployment criterion: any announcement scoring below 60 should be
automatically flagged for re-broadcast or microphone inspection.

Scores below 40 ("Average" or worse) represent recordings where speech
intelligibility is significantly compromised. In a production deployment,
these should trigger automated alerts.

The confidence score provides an additional dimension of operational value.
A high-scoring but low-confidence recording may indicate intermittent
interference (e.g., a passing train intermittently drowning out speech).
An operator should treat low-confidence results with caution regardless
of the absolute score.



================================================================================
8. LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

1. No Reference-Free Absolute Intelligibility Measure.
The system estimates relative quality based on acoustic feature profiles, not
the absolute probability that a listener will understand the semantic content.
MOS (Mean Opinion Score) correlation has not been validated against human
listener studies. The scores are internally consistent but have not been
anchored to an external perceptual benchmark.

2. Mono 16kHz Constraint.
The DSP pipeline requires standardized mono audio at 16 kHz. Stereo
recordings are averaged to mono, potentially discarding useful inter-channel
phase information that indicates reverberation characteristics. Audio at
non-standard sample rates is passed through without proper resampling, which
can cause FFT frequency bin misalignment.

3. Threshold Generalizability.
The scoring thresholds (SF > 0.2, wSNR < 10, ZCR > 0.12) were calibrated
on a synthetic test matrix of five audio profiles, not a labeled real-world
corpus of station recordings. Environmental edge cases — strong echo
chambers, tonal music-over-PA interference, non-English phonetic profiles
with different FER distributions, feedback loops — have not been
systematically tested.

4. No Temporal Granularity.
The final score is a temporal mean across one-second chunks. A five-second
announcement where the first three seconds are clean (score ~85) and the
last two seconds are corrupted (score ~10) would receive a blended score
of approximately 55. The operator cannot identify the temporal location
of the degradation from the single aggregate score.

5. Transcription Dependency.
The Google Speech Recognition API requires internet connectivity and is
subject to rate limiting and occasional service unavailability. In air-
gapped station environments or during network outages, transcription would
not be available. However, the quality score itself is entirely independent
of transcription — it relies only on local DSP computation.

6. Environmental Specificity.
The system was designed for indoor/semi-outdoor PA environments. Fully
outdoor deployments (open-air bus stops, rail yards) may exhibit wind noise
profiles that differ from the training assumptions, potentially causing
ZCR-based false positives.



================================================================================
9. FUTURE WORK
================================================================================

9.1 PERCEPTUAL MOS CALIBRATION

A formal corpus of rated railway announcement recordings should be collected
from 3-5 stations, capturing a range of quality conditions. A panel of 20+
listeners should rate each recording on the ITU-T P.800 five-point MOS
scale. The scoring parameters (sigmoid center, steepness coefficient, power
exponent, dB ceiling, safe zone boundaries) should then be tuned against the
human ratings using regression analysis or Bayesian optimization. This would
transform the system from an internally-consistent scorer to a
perceptually-calibrated predictor.

9.2 DEEP LEARNING SPEECH PRESENCE CLASSIFIER

The P_speech gate, currently based on FER thresholds, could be replaced with
a lightweight binary classifier trained to detect the presence or absence of
human speech. Candidates include:

    - Silero-VAD (Voice Activity Detection): A compact ONNX model (~3 MB)
      that can run without PyTorch at inference time.
    - A custom 3-layer 1D CNN trained on the LibriSpeech corpus with noise
      augmentation from the ESC-50 environmental sound dataset.
    - The SpeechBrain VAD module with quantized ONNX export.

This would make the speech gate robust to tonal content (station chimes,
music) and pseudo-speech noise that happens to concentrate energy in the
300-3400 Hz band.

9.3 PER-SECOND SCORE TIMELINE

The one-second chunking infrastructure is already in place. Extending the
system to emit per-chunk scores as a time series — rather than averaging
them — would produce a quality timeline. Displayed as a line chart on the
frontend dashboard, this would allow operators to identify the exact
temporal segment where quality degraded and request re-broadcast of only
that portion, rather than the entire announcement.

9.4 REAL-TIME STREAMING ARCHITECTURE

Replace the file-upload model with a WebSocket-based streaming pipeline.
Audio captured at the microphone is chunked into one-second buffers and
transmitted to the backend via WebSocket. Each buffer is scored independently
and the result is pushed back to the frontend, producing a continuously
updating live score display. This would enable true real-time monitoring of
PA system output during broadcast.

9.5 ISO/IEC STANDARDS ALIGNMENT

Align the scoring output format with the ISO 7240 series (fire detection
and alarm systems, which include PA quality requirements) and the EN 54-16
standard (voice alarm control and indicating equipment) to enable formal
compliance reporting for infrastructure audits.

9.6 HARDWARE INTEGRATION

Wrap the Python backend in a Docker container with configurable ALSA or
PulseAudio input sources. This allows direct integration with station PA
hardware via professional audio capture interfaces (e.g., Focusrite
Scarlett series, Behringer UMC series) without requiring manual file
upload.



================================================================================
10. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This paper presents the Announcement Quality Analyzer — a practical,
deployable, blind audio quality assessment system designed for public
transportation PA environments.

By combining four DSP features — Windowed SNR, Spectral Flatness, Formant
Energy Ratio, and Zero Crossing Rate — through a calibrated non-linear
scoring pipeline with multiplicative penalty gating, the system produces
score distributions that strongly discriminate between high-quality speech
and degraded audio.

The multiplicative penalty architecture represents a significant design
improvement over additive approaches, producing consistent proportional
score reductions regardless of base score level. The system achieves a
score spread exceeding 85 points between clean studio speech and heavily
corrupted noise-only audio — far surpassing the 10-15 point spread
produced by the initial linear mapping implementation.

Five complete iterations of the scoring model were required to reach this
result. Each iteration revealed a specific mathematical or acoustic flaw:
linear mapping fails to match perceptual curves; subtractive penalties
operate inconsistently across base score levels; raw formant ratios
cannot serve as direct multipliers due to biological vocal tract
limitations; sigmoid centers and normalization constants must be calibrated
to the physical properties of real speech, not to arbitrary round numbers.

The system's full-stack web deployment — including real-time microphone
capture via the MediaRecorder API, interactive waveform visualization via
WaveSurfer.js, and a transparent debug panel exposing all intermediate
scoring parameters — makes it suitable for both operational deployment
and academic demonstration.

The system requires only Python, NumPy, SciPy, and a web browser. It
runs on standard CPU hardware. It is deterministic, explainable, and
produces the same score for the same input every time it is run.

Future work targeting MOS calibration against human listener studies,
deep learning speech presence detection, and per-second temporal score
visualization will further bridge the gap between this prototype and a
production-grade transit quality monitoring system.

   Computed between AQA final scores and human MOS ratings on a labeled

   Target: RMSE < 0.5 MOS units.

   For prediction of Excellent / Good / Average / Poor / Bad categories
   against human-assigned labels. Target: F1 > 0.80.

   To verify that the AQA system preserves relative quality ordering even
   if it does not match absolute MOS values. Target: rho > 0.90.
