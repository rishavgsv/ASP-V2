SLIDE 3 — WHY THIS PROBLEM MATTERS
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Data-driven, authoritative]

Let us quantify the scale of this problem.

India's railway network is the fourth-largest in the world by track length
and the single largest by daily passenger volume. Indian Railways carries
over 22 million passengers per day across approximately 13,000 trains. Those
passengers are served by over 7,000 stations, each equipped with a public
address system.

Accurate, intelligible public announcements are classified as safety-critical
communication. They are the primary mechanism for communicating:

— Platform assignments and last-minute changes
— Train delays and cancellations
— Emergency evacuation instructions
— Security alerts

When these announcements fail — due to poor microphone quality, excessive
ambient noise, speaker distortion, or reverberation in large station halls —
the consequences range from passenger inconvenience to genuine safety
hazards.

Internationally, this problem is well-recognized. The International
Telecommunication Union maintains the G.107 standard, also known as the
E-model, which models voice transmission quality in communication networks.
ITU-T Recommendation P.862, known as PESQ (Perceptual Evaluation of Speech
Quality), provides an objective metric for assessing narrowband telephony
speech.

However, PESQ requires two things that make it impractical for our use case.
First, it needs a clean reference recording — the "ideal" version of the
announcement. In a live station environment, no such reference exists.
Second, it requires specialized laboratory-grade hardware and licensed
software.

Our system eliminates both requirements. It operates in what the audio
engineering community calls a "blind" or "non-intrusive" paradigm — meaning
it evaluates audio quality using only the degraded signal itself, with no
reference, no model weights, and no GPU.

It runs on standard Python with NumPy and SciPy. It could run on a Raspberry
Pi.

SLIDE 4 — EXISTING CHALLENGES
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Analytical, problem-focused]

Before explaining what we built, let us understand why this problem is
technically difficult. There are four core challenges.

CHALLENGE 1: THE ABSENCE OF GROUND TRUTH.

Unlike image classification where you can say "this is a cat" or "this is
not a cat," audio quality has no binary truth. A recording that sounds
"good" in a quiet room sounds completely different in a crowded station
hall. Quality is relative to context — and our system has no context. It
receives a raw audio file and must make a judgment in isolation.

CHALLENGE 2: PERCEPTUAL NON-LINEARITY.

Human hearing does not perceive audio quality on a linear scale. The
difference between a Signal-to-Noise Ratio of 5 decibels and 10 decibels
feels enormous to a listener — the audio transitions from unintelligible
to barely understandable. But the difference between 25 dB and 30 dB is
almost imperceptible — both sound clean.

This means a naive linear scoring function will never align with human
perception. We need mathematically non-linear transformations — sigmoid
curves, logarithmic functions, exponential decays — to map physical
measurements to perceptual scores.

CHALLENGE 3: THE SEPARATION PROBLEM.

This was our single biggest practical challenge during development. Making
a scoring system that produces visually and numerically distinct scores for
clean versus noisy audio is far harder than it appears.

In our first three model iterations, clean audio would score approximately
40 out of 100 and noisy audio would score approximately 30. A 10-point gap
on a 100-point scale is not actionable — an operator looking at a dashboard
cannot quickly distinguish "good" from "bad" when the numbers are that close.

We needed a gap of at least 50 points between clean and noisy audio. Achieving
that gap required five complete rewrites of the scoring mathematics.

CHALLENGE 4: DEPENDENCY CONSTRAINTS.

Industry-grade audio quality models — such as deep neural networks trained
on the NISQA or LibriSpeech corpora — require CUDA-capable GPUs, PyTorch,
and model weight files often exceeding 500 megabytes. We encountered DLL
loading failures with PyTorch in our development environment. Rather than
treating this as a roadblock, we treated it as a design constraint: the
system must work without any deep learning dependencies.

This forced us to rely entirely on classical DSP — and, as we will show,
the results are strong.

SLIDE 5 — OUR SOLUTION OVERVIEW
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Clear, structured]

Our solution is the Announcement Quality Analyzer — a fully blind, non-
intrusive audio evaluation engine.

The system operates in three stages.

STAGE 1: SIGNAL ACQUISITION.

The user provides audio through one of two input methods:
— File upload: The user selects a WAV, MP3, or M4A file from their device.
— Live recording: The user clicks a "Record" button in the web interface,
  speaks into their microphone, and stops when finished. The browser's
  native MediaRecorder API captures the audio directly.

In both cases, the audio is sent as a file upload to our Python backend.

STAGE 2: FEATURE EXTRACTION.

The incoming audio is first standardized — converted to 16kHz mono WAV
format using PyDub — ensuring that all downstream DSP calculations operate
on a consistent signal representation.

The standardized audio is then normalized (peak amplitude scaling to the
range [-1.0, +1.0]) and soft-trimmed (leading and trailing silence removed
with contextual 2000-sample padding to preserve waveform continuity).

The processed audio is then sliced into non-overlapping one-second windows.
For each window, four DSP features are extracted independently:

1. Windowed Signal-to-Noise Ratio (wSNR)
2. Spectral Flatness (SF)
3. Formant Energy Ratio (FER)
4. Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR)

After all windows are processed, the per-window features are averaged to
produce single aggregate values. The standard deviation of the Formant
Energy Ratio across windows is used to derive a Confidence Score — a
measure of how temporally stable the signal is.

STAGE 3: NON-LINEAR SCORING.

The four averaged features are fed into a calibrated mathematical scoring
engine. Each feature is transformed through a perceptually aligned non-linear
function, then combined into a base score (maximum 100 points). Three
multiplicative penalty gates are applied to the base score. The final output
is a deterministic quality score on a 0-to-100 scale.

The result: clean studio audio consistently scores between 85 and 95. Heavy
noise or silence consistently scores below 1.

SLIDE 7 — DSP CONCEPTS USED (DETAILED EXPLANATION)
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Educational, building from intuition to mathematics]

Now let us go deep into the four Digital Signal Processing concepts at the
heart of this system. For each one, I will explain the intuition first, then
the mathematics, then how it maps to audio quality.

----------
CONCEPT 1: WINDOWED SIGNAL-TO-NOISE RATIO (wSNR)
----------

INTUITION:
Imagine you are in a room. Someone speaks a sentence, then pauses. During
the sentence, the room is loud — the speaker's voice dominates. During
the pause, the room is quiet — you hear only the hum of the air conditioner,
the distant chatter of people, the ambient environment.

The difference in loudness between the speech and the silence is what we call
the Signal-to-Noise Ratio. A large SNR means the speech is dramatically
louder than the background — this is a clean recording. A small SNR means
you can barely distinguish the speech from the noise — this is a poor
recording.

MATHEMATICS:
We do not measure SNR on the whole recording at once. That would be easily
fooled — a single loud burst in an otherwise noisy recording would inflate
the average. Instead, we use a windowed approach.

First, we chop the audio into tiny frames of 20 milliseconds each. At a
sample rate of 16,000 Hz, each frame is exactly 320 samples long.

For each frame, we compute the Root Mean Square (RMS) energy:

    E_k = (1/L) * sum(x[n]^2)   for the k-th frame of length L

This gives us an array of energy values — one per frame. We sort this
array from smallest to largest.

The bottom 20% of frames, by energy, represent the quietest moments in the
recording — these are the "noise" frames. The top 20% represent the
loudest moments — these are the "speech" frames.

We compute the ratio:

    SNR = 10 * log10(mean(E_top_20%) / mean(E_bottom_20%))

The result is in decibels. A clean recording typically produces SNR values
of 18 to 25 dB. A noisy recording produces values of 3 to 8 dB.

WHY IT MATTERS:
This is the single most important feature in our system. It directly
measures the fundamental property that makes speech intelligible: dynamic
contrast. Without dynamic contrast, speech is buried in noise and cannot
be understood.

----------
CONCEPT 2: SPECTRAL FLATNESS (SF)
----------

INTUITION:
Every sound has a frequency fingerprint. A pure musical note — like a tuning
fork — puts all its energy at one single frequency. Its spectrum looks like
a sharp spike. White noise, on the other hand, spreads its energy evenly
across all frequencies equally. Its spectrum looks like a flat horizontal
line.

Spectral Flatness measures how "spike-like" versus how "flat" the frequency
spectrum is. A flat spectrum means noise. A spiky spectrum means tonal
content — which is what voiced human speech produces.

MATHEMATICS:
We first convert the audio from the time domain to the frequency domain
using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). The FFT produces a complex-valued
array. We take the magnitude squared of each element to get the power
spectrum.

We then compute two kinds of averages over this power spectrum:

The arithmetic mean (the ordinary average):
    A = (1/N) * sum(P[k])

The geometric mean (the multiplicative average):
    G = exp((1/N) * sum(log(P[k])))

Spectral Flatness is the ratio:
    SF = G / A

Mathematically, the geometric mean is always less than or equal to the
arithmetic mean (this is the AM-GM inequality). The ratio equals 1 only
when all values in the spectrum are identical — which is the definition
of white noise.

For a tonal signal (like speech), the power spectrum has a few very large
peaks and many small values. The geometric mean, being sensitive to small
values, drops dramatically — producing a flatness near 0.

For our scoring engine, we convert this ratio to decibels:
    flatness_dB = -10 * log10(SF)

This linearizes the perceptual scale. A flatness of 0.04 (good speech)
becomes approximately 14 dB. A flatness of 0.35 (noisy) becomes
approximately 4.6 dB.

WHY IT MATTERS:
Spectral Flatness is our hiss and drone detector. If a recording has a
high-pitched electrical hum, a fan running in the background, or broadband
static — the flatness goes up, and the score goes down.

----------
CONCEPT 3: FORMANT ENERGY RATIO (FER)
----------

INTUITION:
Human speech is produced by the vocal cords vibrating and the resulting
sound resonating through the throat, mouth, and nasal cavities. These
resonances create characteristic peaks in the frequency spectrum called
formants. The first three formants (F1, F2, F3) are the features that
allow listeners to distinguish vowels from each other.

Almost all of the intelligible content of speech falls between 300 Hz and
3400 Hz. This is why traditional telephone networks were designed to
transmit only this bandwidth — it is sufficient for understanding speech.

Our system measures what fraction of the total audio energy falls inside
this 300-3400 Hz range.

MATHEMATICS:
After computing the FFT and power spectrum, we create a frequency axis:

    f[k] = k * (sample_rate / N)

We create a boolean mask that is True for all frequency bins between
300 Hz and 3400 Hz:

    mask = (f >= 300) AND (f <= 3400)

We sum the power spectrum values where the mask is True:
    formant_power = sum(P[k] where mask[k] is True)

And sum the total power:
    total_power = sum(P[k] for all k)

The Formant Energy Ratio is:
    FER = formant_power / total_power

For a clean human voice recording, FER typically falls between 0.35 and
0.50. The reason it does not reach 1.0 is that the human voice also
produces energy below 300 Hz (the fundamental frequency of a male voice
is typically around 100-150 Hz) and above 3400 Hz (fricative consonants
like "s" and "f" produce energy up to 8000 Hz).

For environmental noise — fans, wind, traffic rumble — the energy is
distributed more broadly, pulling FER down toward 0.1 or lower.

WHY IT MATTERS:
FER is our speech presence detector. It answers the question: "Is there
actually a human voice in this recording, or is it just ambient noise?"
Without a human voice, the announcement has no content — it should score
near zero regardless of how sonically "clean" it might be.

----------
CONCEPT 4: ZERO CROSSING RATE (ZCR)
----------

INTUITION:
The Zero Crossing Rate counts how many times the audio waveform crosses
the zero-amplitude axis per unit time. Imagine watching the waveform on
an oscilloscope — ZCR is how many times the line bounces above and below
the center.

Clean speech has a moderate ZCR. Voiced segments (vowels like "aah" and
"eee") have low ZCR because the vocal cords produce smooth periodic waves.
Unvoiced segments (consonants like "sss" and "fff") have higher ZCR
because they are turbulent noise.

But extreme ZCR — much higher than any normal speech — indicates problems.
Electrical crackling, microphone clipping, wind buffeting, and digital
artifacts all produce very rapid sign changes.

MATHEMATICS:
    ZCR = (1/N) * count of sign changes in x[n]

We compute this efficiently using:
    zero_crossings = nonzero(diff(x > 0))
    ZCR = len(zero_crossings) / len(x)

Normal speech ZCR typically falls between 0.06 and 0.12. Values above
0.15 strongly indicate distortion. Values above 0.3 indicate severe
corruption — the signal is more static than speech.

WHY IT MATTERS:
ZCR is our distortion alarm. It catches problems that the other three
features might miss — particularly microphone issues, digital clipping,
and wind interference. These are common failure modes in outdoor station
environments.

SLIDE 8 — MATHEMATICAL MODEL BEHIND SCORING
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Precise, walking through equations step by step]

Now for the core mathematics — and this is where the system becomes
genuinely interesting from an engineering standpoint.

The scoring pipeline has two distinct stages: Base Score Computation and
Multiplicative Penalty Application.

STAGE 1: NON-LINEAR BASE SCORE (Maximum 100 points)

The base score is the sum of three independently computed component scores.

COMPONENT A: WINDOWED SNR SCORE (0 to 40 points)

We use a sigmoid function:

    wSNR_score = 40 / (1 + exp(-0.5 * (wSNR - 12)))

Why sigmoid? Because human perception of audio quality follows an S-shaped
curve. The sigmoid function, mathematically, is defined as:

    sigma(x) = 1 / (1 + exp(-x))

This function has three critical properties:
— It asymptotically approaches 0 as x approaches negative infinity.
— It asymptotically approaches 1 as x approaches positive infinity.
— It has its steepest slope at x = 0 — the transition region.

By setting the center at 12 dB and the steepness coefficient at 0.5, we
achieve the following behavior:

— At wSNR = 3 dB (very noisy):   score = 40 / (1 + exp(-0.5 * -9))
                                        = 40 / (1 + exp(4.5))
                                        = 40 / 91.4
                                        = 0.4 points

— At wSNR = 12 dB (moderate):    score = 40 / (1 + exp(0))
                                        = 40 / 2
                                        = 20.0 points

— At wSNR = 22 dB (clean):       score = 40 / (1 + exp(-0.5 * 10))
                                        = 40 / (1 + exp(-5))
                                        = 40 / 1.0067
                                        = 39.7 points

This is exactly the perceptual behavior we want: massive gains in the
middle, near-zero improvement at the extremes.

COMPONENT B: FORMANT ENERGY SCORE (0 to 35 points)

We use a power function:

    clamped_FER = clip(FER / 0.45, 0, 1)
    Formant_score = 35 * clamped_FER^1.5

The division by 0.45 is a biological calibration. Real human speech never
achieves an FER of 1.0 — the sub-fundamental bass energy and high-frequency
fricatives ensure FER maxes out around 0.45-0.50. By normalizing to 0.45,
we allow perfect human speech to achieve the full 35 points.

The exponent of 1.5 makes the score grow super-linearly. At low FER values
(noise-dominant audio), the power function produces very small scores. At
high FER values (speech-dominant audio), it rewards generously. This creates
natural score separation between speech and noise.

COMPONENT C: SPECTRAL FLATNESS SCORE (0 to 25 points)

We use a logarithmic transformation:

    flatness_dB = -10 * log10(clip(SF, 1e-6, 1.0))
    SF_score = 25 * clip(flatness_dB / 30, 0, 1)

Converting to decibels is the natural approach because decibels already
linearize the multiplicative nature of acoustic measurements. The ceiling
of 30 dB was calibrated so that good audio (with SF around 0.04, producing
14 dB) captures approximately 47% of the available 25 points, while very
noisy audio (with SF around 0.35, producing only 4.6 dB) gets nearly
nothing.

BASE TOTAL:
    Base = wSNR_score + Formant_score + SF_score

For clean studio audio, this typically evaluates to approximately 86 points.
For heavy noise, approximately 3 points.

STAGE 2: MULTIPLICATIVE PENALTIES

The base score is then multiplied by three penalty gates. This is
fundamentally different from the subtractive approach we used in earlier
iterations. Subtraction allows a noisy recording to "make up" lost points
if one feature happens to be acceptable. Multiplication does not — a
single bad gate can collapse the entire score.

PENALTY A: ZCR EXPONENTIAL DECAY

    if ZCR <= 0.12:
        P_zcr = 1.0        (no penalty — safe zone for normal speech)
    else:
        P_zcr = exp(-8.0 * (ZCR - 0.12))

The safe zone at 0.12 ensures that natural fricative consonants ("s", "sh",
"f") do not trigger the penalty. Above 0.12, the exponential decay is
steep — at ZCR = 0.18, the multiplier is exp(-8 * 0.06) = exp(-0.48) =
0.62. At ZCR = 0.35, it collapses to exp(-8 * 0.23) = exp(-1.84) = 0.16.

PENALTY B: NOISE TRAPDOOR

    if wSNR < 10 dB:      P_noise = 0.4   (60% instant collapse)
    elif SF > 0.2:         P_noise = 0.5   (50% collapse)
    else:                  P_noise = 1.0   (no penalty)

This is a hard gating function. If the SNR is catastrophically low or the
spectral flatness indicates heavy broadband noise, the score is immediately
halved or worse. This creates the dramatic separation between clean and
noisy audio.

PENALTY C: SPEECH PRESENCE GATE

    P_speech = min(1.0, (FER / 0.35) ^ 0.7)

The exponent of 0.7 makes this a compressive function — it is generous at
moderate FER values (speech present but not dominant) and only punishes
when FER drops very low (no meaningful speech content).

FINAL PIPELINE:

    Final_Score = Base * (0.5 + 0.5 * P_speech) * P_zcr * P_noise

The soft floor of 0.5 in the speech gate is critical. It means that even
if P_speech is zero (no speech detected at all), the base score is reduced
by only 50% from the speech gate — rather than being fully zeroed.

For clean audio with all multipliers at or near 1.0, the final score
equals the base score: approximately 86.

For heavy noise with P_noise = 0.4, P_zcr = 0.05, P_speech = 0.21, the
final score collapses: 3.9 * (0.5 + 0.105) * 0.05 * 0.4 = approximately
0.05 — effectively zero.

This produces the 86-point gap we need.

[NARRATION — Deliberate, line-by-line explanation]

Let us look at the actual source code. I will walk through four critical

----------
SNIPPET 1: FRAME-BASED SNR ESTIMATION
File: processor.py, lines 56-72
----------

    def calculate_snr(self, audio):
        if len(audio) == 0: return 0.0

        frame_len = int(self.sample_rate * 0.02)
        if len(audio) < frame_len: return 5.0

        num_frames = len(audio) // frame_len
        frames = audio[:num_frames * frame_len].reshape(num_frames, frame_len)
        energies = np.mean(frames**2, axis=1)

        sorted_energy = np.sort(energies)
        noise_energy = np.mean(sorted_energy[:max(1, int(0.2 * num_frames))])
        speech_energy = np.mean(sorted_energy[int(0.8 * num_frames):])

        snr = 10 * np.log10((speech_energy + 1e-10) / (noise_energy + 1e-10))
        return float(snr)

Line-by-line:

Line 1: We define the method. It takes the preprocessed audio array.
Line 2: Safety guard — if the audio is empty, return 0 dB.
Line 3: We calculate the frame length as 2% of the sample rate. At 16kHz,
         this is 320 samples — representing 20 milliseconds of audio.
Line 4: If the audio is shorter than one frame, return a default 5 dB.
Line 5: Integer division to compute how many complete frames fit.
Line 6: We truncate the audio to an exact multiple of frame_len, then
         reshape it into a 2D array where each row is one frame. This is a
         zero-copy NumPy operation — extremely efficient.
Line 7: For each frame (each row), we compute the mean squared amplitude.
         This is the RMS energy per frame.
Line 8: We sort the energies from smallest to largest.
Line 9: The bottom 20% of frames by energy — these are the background hum.
Line 10: The top 20% of frames by energy — these are the speech bursts.
Line 11: We compute the ratio in decibels. The epsilon (1e-10) prevents
          log(0) from crashing the system. The result is a single float
          representing the dynamic contrast of the recording.

The key insight: we do NOT compute a single RMS over the entire audio. We
compute per-frame energy, sort it, and compare tails. This makes the
measurement robust to transient noise events.

SNIPPET 2: SPECTRAL FLATNESS VIA FFT

    from scipy.fft import fft
    import warnings
    N = len(chk)

    power_spectrum = yf ** 2
    with warnings.catch_warnings():

Line-by-line:

Line 1: Import the FFT from SciPy. We use SciPy rather than NumPy's FFT
         because SciPy's implementation includes hardware-accelerated
         backends.
Line 2-3: Get the chunk length N.
Line 4: Compute the FFT. fft(chk) returns complex values. np.abs() takes
         the magnitude. [:N//2] takes only the positive-frequency half
         (the FFT is symmetric for real inputs). We add 1e-10 to prevent
         log(0) in the next step.
Line 5: Square the magnitude to get the power spectrum.
Lines 6-7: Suppress RuntimeWarnings that NumPy emits when computing log
            of very small numbers.
Line 8: Compute the ordinary arithmetic average of the power spectrum.
Line 9: Compute the geometric mean in log-space: exp(mean(log(x))). This
         is numerically stable for very large and very small values.
Line 10: Divide geometric by arithmetic mean. By the AM-GM inequality,
          this ratio is always between 0 and 1. Close to 0 means tonal.
          Close to 1 means white noise.

    N = len(chk)
    xf = fftfreq(N, 1 / self.sample_rate)[:N//2]
    band_mask = (xf >= 300) & (xf <= 3400)
    formant_power = np.sum(power_spectrum[band_mask])
    total_power = np.sum(power_spectrum) + 1e-10
    formant_energy_ratio = formant_power / total_power

Line-by-line:

Line 1: fftfreq generates the frequency value (in Hz) corresponding to
         each bin of the FFT output.
Line 2: N is the chunk length.
Line 3: We generate the frequency array and take the positive half. At
         16kHz sample rate with N=16000, this gives us frequencies from
         0 Hz to 8000 Hz with 1 Hz resolution.
Line 4: Create a boolean mask. True for every frequency bin between 300 Hz
         and 3400 Hz. False everywhere else.
Line 5: Sum power spectrum values only where the mask is True — this gives
         us the total energy in the speech band.
Line 6: Sum all power spectrum values — total energy. Add epsilon.
Line 7: The ratio. For clean speech, this is typically 0.35 to 0.50.

SNIPPET 4: THE COMPLETE SCORING PIPELINE
File: inference.py, lines 41-84
----------

    # Base scores
    wsnr_score = 40.0 / (1.0 + math.exp(-0.5 * (windowed_snr - 12.0)))

    clamped_formant = max(0.0, min(1.0, formant_energy_ratio / 0.45))
    formant_score = 35.0 * (clamped_formant ** 1.5)

    flatness = np.clip(spectral_flatness, 1e-6, 1.0)
    flatness_db = -10 * np.log10(flatness)
    sf_score = 25.0 * np.clip(flatness_db / 30.0, 0.0, 1.0)

    base_score = wsnr_score + formant_score + sf_score

    # Penalties
    if zcr <= 0.12:
        p_zcr = 1.0
    else:
        p_zcr = math.exp(-8.0 * (zcr - 0.12))

    if windowed_snr < 10.0:
        p_noise = 0.4
    elif spectral_flatness > 0.2:
        p_noise = 0.5
    else:
        p_noise = 1.0

    p_speech = min(1.0, (formant_energy_ratio / 0.35) ** 0.7)

    final_score = base_score * (0.5 + 0.5 * p_speech) * p_zcr * p_noise

Every single line is an explicit, inspectable mathematical decision. There
are no hidden weights. No black boxes. No matrices of learned parameters.
Every score produced by this system can be traced back to a specific
acoustic property of the input audio. This is the defining advantage of
a DSP-based approach.

SLIDE 12 — RESULTS AND OBSERVATIONS
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Data-driven, table format]

After five complete iterations of the scoring model and extensive calibration,
here are our validated scores against simulated audio profiles:

SCORE DISTRIBUTION TABLE:

    Audio Condition            | wSNR  | FER  | SF   | ZCR  | Final Score | Label
    ----------------------------+-------+------+------+------+-------------+---------
    Clean Studio Recording     | 22 dB | 0.50 | 0.04 | 0.09 |    86.4     | Excellent
    Good Railway Announcement  | 18 dB | 0.42 | 0.08 | 0.11 |    76.9     | Good
    Moderate Ambient Noise     | 13 dB | 0.30 | 0.15 | 0.18 |    27.6     | Poor
    Noisy / Distorted          |  7 dB | 0.12 | 0.35 | 0.35 |     0.5     | Bad
    Heavy Noise, No Speech     |  3 dB | 0.05 | 0.55 | 0.50 |     0.0     | Bad

KEY OBSERVATIONS:

1. The score gap between Clean Studio and Heavy Noise is 86.4 points.
   This is more than sufficient for visual and operational discrimination.

2. The multiplicative gating is responsible for the dramatic collapse
   in noisy profiles. The P_noise trapdoor at 0.4 combined with a low
   P_zcr creates a compounding floor effect — the Gates multiply each
   other, producing near-zero scores for truly corrupted audio.

3. The biggest calibration challenge was the speech presence gate. We
   discovered that human speech acoustics dictate that the Formant Energy
   Ratio maxes out around 0.45-0.50 for real recordings — never reaching
   1.0. Using FER directly as a multiplier (as we did in Method 4)
   inadvertently capped all scores at 50%. The fix — normalizing by 0.35
   and adding the soft floor of (0.5 + 0.5 * P_speech) — resolved this.

4. The sigmoid center for SNR was shifted from 18 dB (initial setting)
   to 12 dB (final setting). At 18 dB, even clean audio could not fully
   saturate the SNR score component. At 12 dB, clean audio at 22 dB
   achieves 39.7 out of 40 points — near-perfect saturation.

SLIDE 13 — LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Honest, technical]

We want to be transparent about the system's current limitations.

LIMITATION 1: MONO 16kHz ONLY.
The DSP pipeline requires conversion to 16kHz mono WAV. Multi-channel audio
is averaged to mono, potentially discarding useful spatial information. Audio
at non-standard sample rates is not properly resampled — the system assumes
16kHz or uses the file as-is, which can skew FFT frequency calculations.

LIMITATION 2: DETERMINISTIC THRESHOLDS.
The scoring thresholds — flatness > 0.2, SNR < 10, ZCR > 0.12 — were
calibrated on a synthetic test matrix, not a labeled real-world corpus. Edge
cases such as strong echo chambers, tonal interference from music-over-PA,
and non-English phonetic profiles have not been systematically tested.

LIMITATION 3: NO TEMPORAL GRANULARITY.
The system averages quality across the full recording. An announcement that
starts clean and degrades into noise halfway through receives a blended
average score. The operator cannot see where in the timeline the quality
dropped.

LIMITATION 4: NO MOS CORRELATION.
We have not validated our scores against a formal Mean Opinion Score study
with human listeners. Our scores measure physical properties of the signal
— not the subjective perception of intelligibility.

LIMITATION 5: TRANSCRIPTION DEPENDENCY.
The Google Speech Recognition API requires internet connectivity and is
rate-limited. In air-gapped station environments, transcription would not
be available. However, the quality score itself does not depend on
transcription — it is purely DSP-based.

SLIDE 14 — FUTURE IMPROVEMENTS
================================================================================

[NARRATION — Forward-looking, practical]

There are three clear engineering paths to making this system production-grade.

IMPROVEMENT 1: PERCEPTUAL MOS CALIBRATION.
Collect a formal corpus of rated railway announcement recordings from
3-5 stations. Have 20+ listeners rate each recording on a 1-5 MOS scale.
Use regression analysis or Bayesian optimization to tune our sigmoid center,
power exponent, and normalization thresholds against the human ratings.

IMPROVEMENT 2: DEEP LEARNING SPEECH PRESENCE CLASSIFIER.
Replace the P_speech gate with a lightweight binary classifier trained to
detect the presence or absence of human speech. Candidates include Silero-VAD,
a 3-layer CNN trained on LibriSpeech with noise augmentation, or the
SpeechBrain VAD module. This would eliminate the FER-based gate entirely and
make the system robust to musical content, tonal alerts, and other non-speech
audio that happens to fall in the 300-3400 Hz range.

IMPROVEMENT 3: PER-SECOND SCORE TIMELINE.
Instead of producing a single aggregate score, emit a score for each one-
second chunk independently. Display this as a time-series curve on the
frontend dashboard. This would allow operators to see exactly where in the
announcement quality degraded, and request re-broadcast of only that
specific segment.
